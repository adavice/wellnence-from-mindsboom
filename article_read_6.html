HOW TO MEASURE SERVING SIZES

Obesity is a growing epidemic as more people than ever are struggling to control their weight. Larger portion sizes are thought to contribute to overeating and unwanted weight gain. Research shows that many factors can affect how much you eat. People eat almost anything they serve themselves. Therefore, controlling portion sizes can help prevent overeating. Here are 9 tips for measuring and controlling the section sizes - both at home and on the go.

1. USE SMALLER TABLEWARE

There is some evidence that the size of plates, spoons and glasses can unknowingly influence how much food a person eats. For example, using large plates can make food appear smaller, often leading to overeating. In one study, people using a large bowl ate 77% more pasta than those using a medium bowl. In another study, nutritionists served themselves 31% more ice with larger bowls and 14.5% more when provided with larger serving spoons.

Interestingly, most of the people who ate more because of large dishes were completely unaware of the change in portion size. Therefore, swapping your usual plate, bowl, or serving spoon for a smaller alternative can help cut down on food and prevent overeating. Most people feel just as full from a smaller dish as from a large one.

2. USE YOUR PLATE AS A SERVING GUIDE

If measuring or weighing food isn't appealing, try your plate or bowl as a serving guide. This can help you determine the optimal macronutrient ratio for a balanced meal.

A rough guide to any meal is:

Vegetables or Salad: Half a plate
High-Quality Protein: Quarter of a plate - this includes meat, poultry, fish, eggs, dairy, tofu, beans, and legumes
Complex Carbohydrates: Quarter of a plate - such as whole grains and starchy vegetables
High-fat foods: Half a tablespoon (7 grams) - including cheese, oils, and butter
Remember, this is a rough guide as people have different nutritional needs. For example, those who are more physically active often need more food. Since vegetables and salad are naturally low in calories but high in fiber and other nutrients, filling these up can help you avoid eating too many calories. If you want extra guidance, some manufacturers sell portion control plates.

3. USE YOUR HANDS AS A SERVICE GUIDE

Another way to measure the correct portion size without measuring tools is to simply use your hands. Since your hands usually correspond to your body size, taller people who need more food tend to have larger hands. A rough guide to any meal is:

Protein-rich foods: One palm-sized serving for women and two palm-sized servings for men - such as meat, fish, poultry, and beans Vegetables and Salads: One fist-sized serving for women and two fist-sized servings for men

Carbohydrate-rich foods: One serving for women and two for men - such as whole grains and starchy vegetables High-fat foods: One thumb-sized serving for women and two for men - such as butter, oil, and nuts

4. ASK FOR HALF PORTION IF YOU GO OUT FOR EATING

Restaurants are notorious for serving large portions. In fact, restaurant portion sizes are on average about 2.5 times larger than standard portion sizes - and up to eight times larger. When eating out, you can always ask for half a portion or a child's dish. This will save you a lot of calories and prevent you from overeating. You can also share a meal with someone or order a starter and side dish instead of a main course. Other tips include ordering a salad or vegetables, asking for sauces and dressings to serve separately, and avoiding all-you-can-eat buffet-style restaurants where it is very easy to indulge.

5. START ALL MEALS WITH A GLASS OF WATER

Drinking a glass of water up to 30 minutes before a meal naturally aids portion control. Refilling with water will make you feel less hungry. If you are well hydrated, you can also distinguish between hunger and thirst. A study in middle-aged and older adults found that drinking 17 ounces (500 ml) of water before each meal resulted in a 44% greater weight loss over 12 weeks, likely due to reduced food intake. Similarly, overweight and obese older adults consumed 500ml of water 30 minutes before a meal 13% fewer calories without trying to make changes. In another study in young men of normal weight, immediately drinking a similar amount of water before a meal resulted in a greater feeling of fullness and decreased food intake. Therefore, a glass of water before every meal can help prevent overeating and aid portion control.

6. TAKE IT SLOWLY

Eating quickly makes you less aware that you are full - and therefore increases the risk of overeating. Since it can take your brain about 20 minutes to register that you are full after eating, slowing down can reduce your overall intake.

IT COMES DOWN TO

Unwanted weight gain can start with large portion sizes. However, there are many practical steps you can take to serve servings. These simple changes have proven successful in reducing portions without compromising on taste or feelings of fullness. For example, measuring your food, using smaller dishes, drinking water before meals, and eating slowly can reduce the risk of overeating. At the end of the day, portion control is a quick fix that will improve your quality of life and possibly prevent cavities.
