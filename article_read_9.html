9 NUTRITIONAL RULES TO HELP YOU LIVE LONGER

Longevity researcher, National Geographic Research Fellow and author of The Blue Zones Solution and The Blue Zones Kitchen Dan Buettner conducted 150 centenarians (100 and older) surveys on their nutrition. Read the nine principles of Blue Zones: what to eat to live longer and be healthy.

Make sure your diet is 90-100% plant-based

Centenarians eat a lot of seasonal vegetables and leafy greens: spinach, kale, beets, turnips, chard and kale, writes CNBC. They dry or pickle the excess. In the Blue Zones, the places where people have the longest life expectancy, legumes, greens, sweet potatoes, whole grains, fruits, nuts and seeds are available all year round.

One of the most important elements in the diet of centenarians is olive oil. Its consumption increases the level of "good" cholesterol and lowers the level of "bad" cholesterol.

On the Greek island of Ikaria, middle-aged people consume about six tablespoons of olive oil daily. This amount reduces the risk of premature death by 50%.

Eat less meat

A 12-year study of 96,000 Americans in California's Loma Linda Blue Zone found that vegans and sand-vegetarians (who eat fish and seafood) live longer. Vegetarians are likely to outlive meat eaters by eight years, scientists say.

Okinawans in Japan have invented what is arguably the best meat substitute: very hard tofu, high in protein and phytoestrogens that help fight cancer.

Eat fish, but not too much

Usually, centenarians eat small and inexpensive fish such as sardines, anchovies and cod. These species are in the middle of the food chain and are not highly exposed to mercury and other chemicals.

Remember to eat your daily dose of legumes

Legumes occupy a special place in the diet of the inhabitants of the "blue zones". Black beans in Nicoya, lentils, peas and white beans in the Mediterranean, and soybeans in Okinawa.

Most centenarians eat at least four times as many beans as the average human - at least half a glass a day. Beans contain more nutrients per gram than any other food on earth. On average, they have 21% protein, 77% complex carbohydrates and only a few percent fat, and a lot of fiber.

Give up sugar

Long-livers consume about the same amount of natural sugar as megapolis residents, but are limited to about a fifth of the amount of added sugar - no more than seven teaspoons per day.

In the period from 1970 to 2000. The amount of added sugar in the world market has grown by 25% (about 22 teaspoons of added sugar per person per day). Typically, sugar is added to soda, yogurt, and sauces.

Save sweets for special occasions, add no more than four teaspoons of sugar a day to coffee or tea, and avoid buying foods that have sugar in the first five ingredients.

Snack on nuts

Eat two small handfuls of nuts a day. This can be almonds, pistachios, brazil nuts, cashews, walnuts, or peanuts. According to a study on nutrition and longevity, people who eat nuts live on average 2-3 years longer.

Eat the right bread

Try to eat only sourdough bread or 100% whole grain bread. In stores, most breads are made from refined white flour, which quickly converts to sugar and raises insulin levels.

In Icaria and Sardinia, bread is made from a variety of whole grains such as wheat, rye or barley. They contain a wide variety of nutrients. Whole grains have significantly more fiber than white flour products.

Some traditional blue zone breads are made from bacteria, such as lactobacilli, which digest starch and gluten, causing the bread to rise. The bread is made with less gluten than bread labeled "gluten-free", with a longer shelf life and a pleasant sour taste.

Drink more water

Avoid soda, even diet soda. Inhabitants of the "blue zones" drink only coffee, tea, water and wine. That's why.

Water. Research shows that water improves blood flow and reduces the likelihood of blood clots.

Coffee. Sardinians, Ikarians and Nikoyans drink coffee. Scientists have linked coffee consumption to a lower risk of dementia and Parkinson's disease.

Tea. Okinawans prefer green tea, which has been shown to reduce the risk of heart disease and certain types of cancer. Ikarians drink a decoction of rosemary, wild sage and dandelion. These herbs have anti-inflammatory properties.

Red wine. People who drink wine in moderation tend to live longer than those who don't drink at all.

Eat Whole Foods

Most centenarians eat whole foods that have not been extensively processed, such as raw fruits and vegetables or crushed grains.

Blue Zones use fermentation - an ancient way of making nutrients bioavailable - in tofu, sourdough bread, wine and pickled vegetables. Long-livers rarely use artificial preservatives: their preparation consists of mixing ingredients without additives.
