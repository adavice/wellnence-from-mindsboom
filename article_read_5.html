A QUICK GUIDE TO INTUITIVE FOOD

Intuitive eating is a philosophy of eating that makes you the expert of your body and its hunger signals. In essence, this is the opposite of a traditional diet. It does not impose guidelines on what to avoid and what or when to eat. Instead, it teaches that you are the best person - the only person - to make those choices. This article is a detailed beginner's guide to intuitive eating.

BASIS

Intuitive eating is an eating style that promotes a healthy attitude to food and body image. The idea is that you should eat when you are hungry and stop when you are full. While this should be an intuitive process, for many people it is not. Relying on diet books and so-called experts on what, when, and how to eat can take you away from trusting your body and its intuition. To eat intuitively, you may need to relearn how to trust your body. To do that, you have to distinguish between physical and emotional hunger:

Physical hunger. This biological urge tells you to replenish nutrients. It builds up gradually and has various signals, such as a growling stomach, fatigue, or irritability. It is satisfying when you eat food. Emotional hunger. This is driven by emotional need. Sadness, loneliness, and boredom are some of the feelings one can crave for food, often comfort food. Eating then causes guilt and self-hatred.

10 MAIN PRINCIPLES

In their book on intuitive eating, Tribole and Resch describe 10 basic principles of philosophy.

1. REFUSE THE NUTRITIONAL MENTALITY

The diet mindset is the idea that there is a diet that will work for you. Intuitive eating is the anti-diet.

2. HONOR YOUR HUNGER

Hunger is not your enemy. Respond to your early signs of hunger by feeding your body. If you let yourself get too hungry, you will likely overeat.

3. MAKE PEACE WITH FOOD

Summon a truce in the war with food. Lose ideas about what to eat or what not to eat.

4. CHALLENGE THE FOOD POLICY

Food is not good or bad and you are not good or bad for what you eat or not eat. Challenge thoughts that tell you differently.

5. RESPECT YOUR FULLNESS

Just as your body tells you when it is hungry, it also tells you when it is full. When you feel you've had enough, listen for the cues of comfortable fullness. Check in with yourself while eating to see how the food tastes and how hungry or full you feel.

6. DISCOVER THE SATISFACTION FACTOR

Make your dining experience enjoyable. Eat a meal that is good for you. Sit down to eat it. If you make eating a pleasurable experience, you may need less food to satisfy you.

7. HONOR YOUR FEELINGS WITHOUT USING FOOD

Emotional eating is a strategy for dealing with feelings. Look for ways unrelated to food to deal with your feelings, such as walking, meditating, keeping a journal, or calling a friend. Become aware of the times when a feeling you might call hunger is really based on emotion.

8. RESPECT YOUR BODY

Rather than criticizing your body for how it looks and what you perceive is wrong with it, recognize it as capable and beautiful just the way it is.

9. EXERCISE - FEEL THE DIFFERENCE

Find ways to move your body that you enjoy. Shift the focus from losing weight to feeling energized, strong and alive.

10. HONOR YOUR HEALTH - SOFT FOOD

The food you eat should taste good and make you feel good. Remember, it is your overall diet that determines your health. One meal or snack will not make or break your health.

BENEFITS

Research on this topic is still growing and is largely focused on women. So far, studies have linked intuitive eating to healthier psychological attitudes, lower body mass index (BMI) and weight maintenance - although not weight loss. One of the main benefits of intuitive eating is better mental health. Participants in intuitive eating studies improved their self-esteem, body image and overall quality of life while experiencing less depression and anxiety. Intuitive eating interventions also have good retention, meaning that people are more likely to stick with the program and continue to practice the behavioral changes than they would be on a diet. Other studies have looked at women's eating behaviors and attitudes and found that those who show more signs of intuitive eating are less likely to have disordered eating.

SUMMARY

Emerging research suggests that intuitive eating is linked to a healthier attitude to food and self-image, and it can be learned through interventions.
